Medical transcription involves listening to dictated recordings from physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and other healthcare providers and converting spoken medical information into accurate written documentation for patient records. A standard transcription report may include patient demographics, date of service, provider name, medical record number, chief complaint, history of present illness, physical examination findings, diagnostic impressions, and treatment plans. Common report types include history and physical (H&P), operative reports, discharge summaries, consultation notes, progress notes, emergency department reports, radiology reports, and pathology reports. Transcriptionists must be proficient in medical terminology, anatomy, pharmacology, abbreviations, punctuation, grammar, and formatting conventions used in electronic health records (EHR) systems. A typical dictated sentence might include phrases such as "The patient is a 54-year-old male presenting with chest pain radiating to the left arm" or "No acute distress noted on examination." Medical transcription requires careful attention to detail because even minor errors in dosage, laterality, or timing can significantly affect patient care outcomes. Providers may dictate medication instructions such as "Take 1 tablet PO BID x 10 days" or "Administer 500 mg IV every 6 hours as needed for pain." Abbreviations commonly encountered include BP for blood pressure, HR for heart rate, RR for respiratory rate, T for temperature, and O2 sat for oxygen saturation. Additional shorthand may include NPO (nothing by mouth), PRN (as needed), QD (once daily), BID (twice daily), TID (three times daily), and QID (four times daily). Transcriptionists often encounter numeric data such as "BP 138/82 mmHg," "HR 92 bpm," "RR 18 breaths/min," and "Temp 98.6°F (37°C)." Medical documentation must maintain HIPAA compliance, ensuring patient confidentiality and secure handling of protected health information (PHI). Reports are typically formatted using standardized templates with headings, subheadings, and consistent spacing for clarity and readability. A history and physical examination may include sections such as HEENT (head, eyes, ears, nose, throat), cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, musculoskeletal, neurological, and integumentary systems. Dictated physical exam findings might state "Lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally, no wheezes, rales, or rhonchi noted" or "Heart regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs, rubs, or gallops." Operative reports describe surgical procedures in detail, including preoperative diagnosis, postoperative diagnosis, anesthesia type, surgical procedure performed, estimated blood loss (EBL), and complications. A surgical dictation might read "Procedure performed: laparoscopic cholecystectomy with intraoperative cholangiogram under general anesthesia." Pathology reports include gross description, microscopic findings, and final diagnosis, often referencing tissue samples labeled with specimen identifiers such as "Specimen A," "Specimen B," or accession numbers like "Accession #2026-014578." Radiology reports document imaging studies such as X-ray, CT scan, MRI, ultrasound, PET scan, and fluoroscopy, including impressions and clinical correlations. A radiology impression might state "Findings consistent with acute right lower lobe pneumonia. Recommend clinical correlation." Transcriptionists must be familiar with anatomical terminology such as proximal, distal, anterior, posterior, medial, lateral, superior, and inferior. Pharmacological references often include medication names like acetaminophen, ibuprofen, amoxicillin, azithromycin, lisinopril, metformin, and warfarin. Dosage instructions may include units such as mg, mcg, mL, units, and IV rates expressed as "mL/hr" or "mcg/kg/min." Laboratory values frequently appear in dictation, including WBC count 8.4 x10^3/µL, hemoglobin 13.2 g/dL, hematocrit 40%, platelet count 250,000/mm³, sodium 140 mEq/L, potassium 4.2 mEq/L, and glucose 110 mg/dL. Medical transcription software may include playback controls, foot pedal integration, timestamping features, voice recognition editing, and template management tools. Accuracy standards require transcriptionists to achieve high error-free rates, often exceeding 98-99% depending on institutional requirements. Quality assurance processes may include peer review, editor correction, and physician verification of finalized reports. Emergency department dictations often contain urgent language such as "Patient arrived via EMS," "Level 1 trauma activation," "STEMI alert," or "stroke protocol initiated." Progress notes typically follow SOAP format: Subjective, Objective, Assessment, and Plan, with concise documentation of daily patient status. A SOAP note might include "S: Patient reports mild improvement in pain. O: Vital signs stable. A: Postoperative recovery progressing well. P: Continue current management." Consultation reports involve specialist evaluations such as cardiology, neurology, orthopedics, gastroenterology, pulmonology, and infectious disease. A cardiology consult might state "Echocardiogram reveals ejection fraction of 55-60% with no regional wall motion abnormalities." Neurology dictation may include findings such as "Cranial nerves II-XII grossly intact, no focal neurological deficits observed." Orthopedic reports may describe fractures using terms like "comminuted," "displaced," "non-displaced," "spiral fracture," or "greenstick fracture." Medical transcriptionists must understand abbreviations for diagnostic tests such as EKG/ECG, EEG, EMG, ABG, CBC, CMP, UA, and BMP. Electrocardiogram interpretations may include phrases like "normal sinus rhythm," "atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response," or "no acute ST-T wave changes noted." Imaging abbreviations may include CXR (chest X-ray), CT (computed tomography), MRI (magnetic resonance imaging), and US (ultrasound). Discharge summaries document hospital course, procedures performed, medications on discharge, follow-up instructions, and condition at discharge such as "stable," "improved," or "guarded." A discharge instruction might read "Follow up with primary care physician in 7-10 days. Return to ER if symptoms worsen." Transcriptionists often encounter dictated corrections such as "scratch that," "correction," or "repeat last sentence" during audio review. Voice files may include background noise, multiple speakers, medical jargon, or unclear dictation requiring careful listening and context interpretation. Editing skills are essential for correcting grammar, punctuation, and formatting while preserving clinical accuracy. Medical terminology frequently includes Latin-based terms such as "status post (s/p)," "rule out (r/o)," "history of present illness (HPI)," and "review of systems (ROS)." A review of systems may include positive or negative findings such as "denies fever, chills, nausea, vomiting, or shortness of breath." Pediatric dictations often include age-specific language such as "term newborn," "well-child visit," or "growth and development appropriate for age." Obstetric and gynecologic reports may include terms like gravida (G), para (P), estimated gestational age (EGA), last menstrual period (LMP), and prenatal care details. A pregnancy note might read "G2P1 female at 28 weeks gestation presenting for routine prenatal follow-up." Psychiatric documentation may include mental status examination findings such as mood, affect, thought process, thought content, insight, judgment, and orientation. A mental status exam might state "Patient alert and oriented x3, mood euthymic, affect congruent, no evidence of hallucinations or delusions." Behavioral health dictation may include diagnoses such as depression, anxiety disorder, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, PTSD, or substance use disorder. Medical transcriptionists must maintain consistency in spelling medical terms, often referencing approved medical dictionaries or style guides such as Stedman's or Dorland's. Formatting rules may require capitalization of section headings, correct abbreviation usage, and standardized punctuation for clarity. Numeric formatting must remain consistent, such as writing "0.5 mg" instead of ".5 mg" to prevent medication errors. Some institutions require SI units, while others use conventional units depending on regional standards. Transcriptionists may encounter dictation such as "Insert Foley catheter size 16 French" or "Start IV normal saline at 75 mL per hour." Surgical instruments may be referenced in operative dictation, including scalpel, forceps, retractors, clamps, sutures, and electrocautery devices. Wound descriptions may include terms like "clean," "dry," "intact," "erythematous," "purulent drainage," or "well approximated edges." Postoperative instructions often include wound care, activity restrictions, medication schedules, and signs of infection such as redness, swelling, or fever. Medical transcription professionals must maintain confidentiality, accuracy, efficiency, and adherence to legal and ethical documentation standards. Continuing education may involve updates in medical terminology, transcription software, healthcare regulations, and evolving electronic documentation systems. Overall, medical transcription serves as a critical link between spoken clinical observations and accurate written medical records that support diagnosis, treatment, billing, and continuity of patient care across healthcare systems.
