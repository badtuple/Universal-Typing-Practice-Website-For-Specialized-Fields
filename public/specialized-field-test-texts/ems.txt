Emergency Medical Services (EMS) personnel provide pre-hospital emergency care, rapid assessment, stabilization, and transport of patients experiencing medical, traumatic, environmental, or psychiatric emergencies in both urban and rural settings. EMS systems include Emergency Medical Dispatch (EMD), Basic Life Support (BLS), Advanced Life Support (ALS), paramedics, emergency medical technicians (EMTs), and critical care transport teams working under medical direction from physicians. A typical EMS call begins with dispatch receiving a 911 report, followed by unit assignment, response mode determination (emergent or non-emergent), and scene arrival with situational awareness and scene safety evaluation.

Upon arrival, EMS providers perform a primary assessment using the ABCDE approach: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, and Exposure. The patient's airway is assessed for patency, obstruction, stridor, or gurgling, while breathing is evaluated through respiratory rate, effort, lung sounds, and oxygen saturation (SpO2). Circulation assessment includes pulse quality, skin color, temperature, capillary refill time, and active bleeding control. Disability assessment includes neurological status using the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS), pupil response, and level of consciousness (LOC). Exposure involves fully assessing the patient while preventing hypothermia and maintaining dignity.

Vital signs are documented frequently, including blood pressure (BP), heart rate (HR), respiratory rate (RR), temperature (T), SpO2, and blood glucose level (BGL). Example vitals may include BP 142/88 mmHg, HR 118 bpm, RR 24 breaths/min, SpO2 92% on room air, T 99.4°F (37.4°C), and BGL 110 mg/dL. Cardiac rhythm interpretation may involve sinus rhythm, sinus tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia (VT), or asystole. EMS units often carry cardiac monitors/defibrillators capable of 3-lead or 12-lead ECG acquisition.

Common EMS medications include oxygen (O2), aspirin 324 mg PO, nitroglycerin 0.4 mg SL, albuterol nebulizer treatments, epinephrine 1:1,000 IM, naloxone (Narcan), glucose 10-25 g IV/PO, and atropine in specific bradycardic protocols. Medication administration follows the "rights": right patient, right medication, right dose, right route, right time, and right documentation.

Trauma assessments include mechanism of injury (MOI), such as motor vehicle collisions (MVC), falls, penetrating trauma (gunshot wounds GSW, stab wounds SW), burns, crush injuries, and blast injuries. Scene size-up includes hazard identification, personal protective equipment (PPE) usage, number of patients, need for additional resources, and extrication requirements. Spinal precautions may include cervical collar application, long spine board use, or selective immobilization protocols depending on local guidelines.

Airway management techniques include head-tilt chin-lift, jaw thrust maneuver, suctioning, oropharyngeal airway (OPA), nasopharyngeal airway (NPA), bag-valve-mask (BVM) ventilation, supraglottic airway devices, and endotracheal intubation for advanced providers. Oxygen delivery devices include nasal cannula (NC) at 1-6 L/min, simple face mask at 6-10 L/min, non-rebreather mask (NRB) at 10-15 L/min, and CPAP/BiPAP for respiratory distress cases.

Respiratory emergencies may involve asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), pulmonary edema, pneumonia, anaphylaxis, or pneumothorax. EMS treatment may include bronchodilators, oxygen therapy, assisted ventilations, or needle decompression for tension pneumothorax using 14-10 gauge catheters placed in the 2nd intercostal space midclavicular line or 5th intercostal space anterior axillary line depending on protocol.

Cardiac emergencies include chest pain, acute coronary syndrome (ACS), myocardial infarction (MI), congestive heart failure (CHF), and cardiac arrest. Chest pain protocols may include aspirin administration, nitroglycerin, oxygen titration, and rapid transport to a STEMI-capable facility. Time-sensitive metrics such as "door-to-balloon time" and "scene time under 10 minutes" are critical performance benchmarks.

Neurological emergencies include stroke, transient ischemic attack (TIA), seizures, hypoglycemia, and altered mental status (AMS). Stroke assessments may include Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale (CPSS) or FAST exam (Face, Arms, Speech, Time). EMS providers may document symptom onset time as "Last Known Well (LKW) at 14:30 hrs."

Pediatric EMS care uses weight-based dosing, often calculated using Broselow tape or estimated weight formulas. Pediatric patients require age-appropriate airway equipment sizes, smaller IV catheters (24g-22g), and specialized emotional support techniques for caregivers and children.

IV access procedures include peripheral IV insertion in veins such as the cephalic, basilic, or dorsal hand veins using catheter sizes ranging from 18g-22g for adults. Intraosseous (IO) access may be used in critical patients when IV access is unsuccessful, often in the proximal tibia or humeral head.

EMS documentation includes patient care reports (PCR), electronic charting systems, narrative documentation, SOAP format (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan), and trauma/medical checklists. A sample narrative may read: "Upon arrival, patient found sitting upright in moderate respiratory distress, speaking in short sentences, with audible wheezing noted bilaterally."

Transport decisions involve determining the appropriate facility type, such as trauma center level I-IV, stroke center, cardiac cath lab facility, or pediatric specialty hospital. Destination decisions are guided by patient condition, transport time, and hospital capabilities.

Radio communication includes structured reports such as the MIST format: Mechanism of injury/medical complaint, Injuries/findings, Signs/vitals, and Treatment given. Example radio report: "Medic 12 en route to General Hospital, 45-year-old male, chest pain, BP 160/100, HR 110, 12-lead showing ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF, ETA 8 minutes."

EMS operations may involve mutual aid agreements, mass casualty incident (MCI) response, triage tagging systems (red, yellow, green, black), and Incident Command System (ICS) structure. Triage categories prioritize patients based on survivability and urgency of care.

Environmental emergencies include hypothermia, hyperthermia, heat stroke, frostbite, drowning, and toxic inhalation. Treatment may include active warming, cooling measures, oxygen therapy, and rapid transport.

Toxicology emergencies involve opioid overdose, carbon monoxide poisoning, alcohol intoxication, pesticide exposure, or accidental ingestion. Naloxone administration may be repeated every 2-3 minutes if respiratory depression persists.

Psychiatric emergencies may include suicidal ideation (SI), homicidal ideation (HI), psychosis, agitation, or behavioral crises. EMS providers ensure scene safety, de-escalation techniques, and appropriate law enforcement coordination when necessary.

Obstetric emergencies include childbirth, postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia, and complications during labor. EMS providers may assist with delivery, clamp and cut umbilical cords, monitor APGAR scores at 1 and 5 minutes, and manage neonatal airway and warmth.

Normal APGAR scoring includes Appearance, Pulse, Grimace, Activity, and Respiration, each scored 0-2 for a maximum of 10. A newborn may require suctioning, stimulation, or assisted ventilations using a neonatal BVM.

Pediatric and adult CPR follows current AHA guidelines with compression rates of 100-120/min and compression depth of at least 2 inches for adults. Ratios may include 30:2 for single rescuer or 15:2 for pediatric two-rescuer CPR.

Defibrillation energy levels vary depending on device type, such as biphasic defibrillators delivering 120-200 joules for adult cardiac arrest protocols. Rhythm checks occur every 2 minutes during resuscitation cycles.

EMS quality improvement (QI) programs review call performance, response times, airway success rates, medication accuracy, and documentation completeness. Key performance indicators (KPIs) include response time < 8 minutes, scene time efficiency, and ROSC (return of spontaneous circulation) rates.

EMS culture emphasizes teamwork, situational awareness, safety, continuous education, and emotional resilience. Providers frequently encounter high-stress environments requiring rapid decision-making under uncertain and dynamic conditions.

Standard EMS shift operations may include 12-hour, 24-hour, or 48-hour shifts with station duties, equipment checks, ambulance readiness inspections, and continuing education training modules.

Equipment inventory includes trauma bags, airway kits, cardiac monitors, suction units, oxygen cylinders, spinal immobilization devices, splints, and hemorrhage control supplies such as tourniquets and hemostatic gauze.

Tourniquet application may be labeled with time of placement such as "TQ applied 15:42 hrs" to ensure proper documentation and reassessment protocols.

Hemorrhage control strategies include direct pressure, wound packing, pressure dressings, and rapid transport for surgical intervention when indicated.

EMS professionals often conclude reports with statements such as "Patient transferred care to ED staff without incident" or "No further changes noted during transport."

Continuous training includes Advanced Cardiac Life Support (ACLS), Pediatric Advanced Life Support (PALS), Prehospital Trauma Life Support (PHTLS), and National Registry EMT/Paramedic certification standards.

EMS practice integrates clinical knowledge, technical skill, communication, and rapid critical thinking to deliver time-sensitive emergency care in diverse and unpredictable environments, often with life-saving impact within minutes of patient contact.
